## Part 3

## Chapter 8: Research

### How long should this take?

Research should take about **15 minutes before each informational interview**. I do not need to know everything. I just need to know enough to ask good questions.

### What are the three movements of an Informational Interview?

The three parts are:

1. **Small Talk**
   Start with a friendly conversation.

2. **Main Discussion**
   Ask questions and listen carefully.

3. **Next Steps**
   Thank them and ask what I should do next.

### What is the 80/20 rule?

The 80/20 rule means the other person should talk more than me. I should listen about **80%** of the time and talk about **20%** of the time.

### What research activities should I accomplish before the interview?

Before the interview, I should:

* Look at the person’s LinkedIn profile
* Look at the company website
* Search the company on Google
* Read some recent news about the company
* Prepare simple questions
* Practice a short introduction about myself

### What are the Big Four?

The Big Four are four common questions I should be ready for:

1. Tell me about yourself.
2. Why are you interested in this company?
3. Why are you interested in this job?
4. Why are you interested in this field?

### What is the FIT mode?

FIT mode means checking if the company, job, or field is a good match for me. I should think about whether it fits my skills, goals, and interests.

## Chapter 9: Discuss

### What is the Ben Franklin effect?

The Ben Franklin effect means people may like helping you after they do a small favor for you. In job searching, this means it is better to ask for advice, not directly ask for a job.

### What is TIARA?

TIARA is a way to ask good questions during an informational interview.

* **T — Trends**
  Ask what is changing in the field.

* **I — Insights**
  Ask what they learned from their own experience.

* **A — Advice**
  Ask what advice they have for you.

* **R — Resources**
  Ask what websites, books, tools, or certifications they recommend.

* **A — Assignments**
  Ask what you should do next.

## Chapter 10: Follow-up

### Outline the Harvest Cycle

The Harvest Cycle is the follow-up process after the interview.

1. Send a thank-you message.
2. Write down what I learned.
3. Do the action step they suggested.
4. Send them an update later.
5. Keep in touch on LinkedIn.
6. Ask if they know anyone else I can talk to.

## Summary

Prioritization can improve my job search because it helps me focus on the most important things first. Job searching can feel overwhelming because there are many jobs, companies, and people to contact. If I do not have a plan, I may spend a lot of time applying to random jobs and not get many results.

The 2-Hour Job Search method helps me use my time better. Instead of applying everywhere, I can first choose companies and people that match my goals. For example, I can focus on BYU-I alumni, cybersecurity professionals, and people who work at companies I am interested in. This makes my LinkedIn connections more useful because I am not just adding random people.

Prioritization also helps me prepare better for informational interviews. If I research the person and company before the meeting, I can ask better questions. I can ask about trends, advice, resources, and next steps. This makes the conversation more helpful and professional.

Follow-up is also important. If I write down what I learned and send a thank-you message, I can build a better relationship with the person. If they give me advice, I should try to follow it and update them later.

Overall, prioritization makes my job search more organized and less stressful. It helps me focus on people and opportunities that fit me. It also helps me build real connections, not just submit applications online. This can make my job search stronger and more successful.

### Opening

I can start by saying:

“Thank you for meeting with me today. I really appreciate your time. I am a cybersecurity student at BYU-Idaho, and I am trying to learn more about this field.”

---

### Questions to Ask

**Trends**

* What changes are you seeing in this field?
* What skills are becoming more important?

**Insights**

* What do you like about your job?
* What was hard when you first started?

**Advice**

* What advice would you give to a student like me?
* What should I work on before graduation?

**Resources**

* Are there any websites, tools, or certifications you recommend?
* Are there any groups or communities I should join?

**Assignments**

* What should I do next to become a better candidate?
* Is there one skill or project I should focus on?

---

### Closing

I can end by saying:

“Thank you again for your time. Your advice was really helpful. I will work on [specific advice]. Would it be okay if I update you later?”

I can also ask:

“Is there anyone else you recommend I talk to?”

---

### After the Interview

After the interview, I will:

* Send a thank-you message
* Write down important notes
* Do the next step they suggested
* Send an update later
