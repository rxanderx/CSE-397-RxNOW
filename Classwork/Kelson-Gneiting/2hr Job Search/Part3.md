**Part 3**

**Chapter 8: Research**
* How long should this take?

About 15 minutes per informational interview prep cycle.

* What are the three movements of an Informational Interview?

1. Small talk/opening rapport
2. Main discussion (questions and learning)
3. Next steps and close

* What is the 80/20 rule?

The other person should talk about 80% of the time and I should talk about 20%. My goal is to learn, not dominate the conversation.

* What research activities should I accomplish before the interview?

* Review the contact's LinkedIn profile and career path.
* Scan the company website and recent announcements.
* Search recent company or industry news.
* Prepare 5-7 focused questions tied to my ML Engineer goals.
* Prepare a brief intro about who I am and what I am pursuing.

* What are the Big Four?

1. Tell me about yourself.
2. Why this company?
3. Why this role?
4. Why this field?

* What is the FIT mode?

FIT mode is evaluating whether the company, team, and role are a fit for my skills, interests, and long-term direction. For me, that means checking alignment with machine learning engineering work, not just any software position.

**Chapter 9: Discuss**
* What is the Ben Franklin effect?

The Ben Franklin effect means people are often more likely to help you again after helping you once. In job search terms, asking for advice can build a relationship more naturally than asking directly for a job.

* What is TIARA?

TIARA is a structure for interview questions:

* Trends - what is changing in the field.
* Insights - what they have learned from experience.
* Advice - what they recommend I do next.
* Resources - tools, courses, people, and certifications.
* Assignments - concrete next actions I should take.

**Chapter 10: Follow-up**
* Outline the Harvest Cycle

1. Send a thank-you message within 24 hours.
2. Document what I learned while it is still fresh.
3. Complete one action item they suggested.
4. Send a short update later showing I acted on advice.
5. Stay connected and ask for one additional introduction if appropriate.

**Summary**
A short reflection (250-300 words) on how prioritization can improve your job search.

Prioritization is the difference between being busy and actually making progress. When I treat every company and every role the same, I burn effort fast and get low-quality outcomes. But when I rank opportunities by fit, I can focus on conversations and applications that move me closer to my real career goal, which is becoming an ML Engineer.

For me, that means filtering opportunities based on whether they involve machine learning systems, data pipelines, model deployment, or related backend engineering. If a role does not connect to that direction, it drops lower in priority unless I need a short-term stepping stone. This mindset helps me avoid random applications that only look good on paper.

Part 3 also made me think more clearly about informational interviews. A focused 15-minute research pass before each conversation gives me better questions and better responses. Using TIARA keeps the discussion practical and keeps me from wasting the other person's time. The 80/20 rule also helps because I can learn faster by listening instead of trying to impress.

Follow-up is another priority lesson. Without a system, good conversations fade quickly. The Harvest Cycle gives me a repeatable method: thank-you, action, update, and continued contact. That process turns a single meeting into a real professional relationship.

Overall, prioritization helps me stay intentional. I am not just job hunting; I am building a path toward ML Engineering with better decisions, better conversations, and better use of limited time.
